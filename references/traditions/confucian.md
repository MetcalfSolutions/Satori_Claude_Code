# Confucian / East Asian Relational Ethics — Deep Reference

**Selection guide:** `traditions-quickref.md` | **Load when:** deeper depth needed on Confucian ethics
**Cultural care:** Not mere authoritarianism — a tradition deeply concerned with mutual obligation and human flourishing in community.

---

## Core Concepts
- **Ren (仁)** — Benevolence, humaneness. The quality of being fully human in relationship. "To love others."
- **Li (禮)** — Ritual propriety; the forms of relationship that enable flourishing. The way you greet, mourn, celebrate — these forms carry meaning.
- **Xiao (孝)** — Filial piety. The weight and beauty of family obligation. Not blind obedience but recognition of a real debt.
- **The Five Relationships** — Each carries mutual responsibilities: ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend.
- **Junzi (君子)** — The exemplary person. Virtue as cultivation, not achievement.
- **The Relational Self** — Identity is constituted by relationships, not by individual autonomy. You are a self *made of* relationships.
- **Zhongyong (中庸)** — The Doctrine of the Mean. Dynamic balance, not mediocrity.

## Key Figures
- **Confucius** — The *Analects*: short, pithy, conversational. Wisdom lives in dialogue and example.
- **Mencius** — Human nature is fundamentally good — the "four sprouts" that grow into virtue with cultivation.
- **Wang Yangming** — "The unity of knowledge and action" — if you truly know what is right and don't do it, you don't actually know it.

## Key Texts

## When to Draw On This
- Family obligation vs. individual desire
- Questions of duty, role, and social responsibility
- Someone whose moral framework is relational rather than individualistic
- The tension between personal freedom and relational fidelity
- Someone from an East Asian cultural background whose ethical framework should be met on its own terms

## Sample Language
> *"There's a way of thinking about this that doesn't start with 'what do I want?' but with 'what does this relationship ask of me?' — and that's not a lesser question."*
> *"The tension between what you owe your family and what you want for yourself — that's not a problem to solve. It's the territory of being a person who takes both things seriously."*
