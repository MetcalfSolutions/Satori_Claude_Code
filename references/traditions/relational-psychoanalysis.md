## Core Concepts
- **The Intersubjective Field** — Meaning is co-created between two people. No "objective observer" in a relationship — both parties are participating in and shaped by the field between them.
- **Mutual Recognition** — Jessica Benjamin: the fundamental need to be seen by another subject — not just an object that serves our needs, but a full person whose perspective matters.
- **Enactments** — Moments where old relational patterns are unconsciously played out in the present. Neither party fully aware — both performing roles from earlier relational templates.
- **Thirdness** — The capacity to hold the other's perspective alongside one's own. The "third" that emerges between two subjectivities. The developmental achievement of mature relating.
- **The Relational Unconscious** — The unspoken rules, roles, and expectations governing a relationship from beneath the surface.
- **Self-States** — Philip Bromberg: the self is not unitary but composed of multiple self-states that shift in different relational contexts. Dissociation is a failure to hold these states together.

## Key Figures
- **Jessica Benjamin** — *The Bonds of Love*, *Beyond Doer and Done To*. On mutual recognition.
- **Stephen Mitchell** — *Relationality*. Founding figure of relational psychoanalysis.
- **Philip Bromberg** — *Standing in the Spaces*. On dissociation and multiple self-states.
- **Donnel Stern** — *Unformulated Experience*. What has never been thought or said.

## When to Draw On This
- Understanding what happens *between* people — the unconscious choreography of relationships
- Someone who keeps recreating the same relational dynamic across different relationships
- The need for mutual recognition as core human hunger — being truly seen
- When the relational pattern is visible in the current conversation in real-time

## Sample Language
> *"I wonder if what's happening between you and him is a version of something that happens in a lot of your relationships — a kind of choreography that neither of you is fully choosing but both of you are performing."*
> *"What you're describing isn't just about being understood — it's about being seen. Genuinely seen by someone who is also a full person."*
