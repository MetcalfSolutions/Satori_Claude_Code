## Core Concepts
- **Dharma (धर्म)** — Duty, cosmic order, one's path and purpose. Highly contextual — what is right depends on who you are and where you stand. Central question of the Bhagavad Gita.
- **Karma (कर्म)** — Action and its consequences. Not cosmic punishment but the principle that actions shape who we become.
- **Karma Yoga** — The yoga of action. Perform right action without attachment to results. One of the most useful frameworks for the gap between effort and outcome.
- **Atman / Brahman** — Individual self (Atman) and universal self (Brahman). "Tat Tvam Asi" — "Thou art That."
- **Maya (माया)** — The veil of illusion; taking the constructed for the real.
- **Moksha (मोक्ष)** — Liberation; freedom from repetitive patterns. Awakening within life.
- **The Gunas** — Three qualities: Sattva (clarity, harmony), Rajas (activity, restlessness), Tamas (inertia, resistance).
- **Bhakti (भक्ति)** — Devotion as spiritual path. Love directed toward the ultimate.

## Key Figures
- **Krishna** — Teacher in the Bhagavad Gita. Counsel to Arjuna on acting from dharma without attachment.
- **Ramana Maharshi** — Self-inquiry. "Who am I?" as the only question that matters.
- **Nisargadatta Maharaj** — *I Am That*. Direct, uncompromising. "You are not what you think you are."

## Key Texts
- *Bhagavad Gita* — Eknath Easwaran's translation is most accessible.
- *Upanishads* — The philosophical core. Easwaran's translation recommended.
- *I Am That* (Nisargadatta Maharaj) — Conversations on non-dual awareness.

## When to Draw On This
- Duty vs. desire — what someone feels called to do vs. what they want
- Questions of purpose and calling (dharma)
- Attachment to outcomes — the gap between effort and result
- Acting without certainty — doing what seems right without knowing the outcome
- Someone from a Hindu background whose framework deserves to be met on its own terms
