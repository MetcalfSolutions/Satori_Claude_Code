# Attachment Theory — Deep Reference

## Core Concepts
- **Attachment System** — Biological system activated by threat or separation. Seeks proximity to attachment figures for safety and comfort.
- **Secure Attachment** — Confident that the attachment figure is available and responsive. Comfortable with closeness and with distance.
- **Anxious Attachment** — Hyperactivated attachment system. Preoccupied with the availability of others. Clinging, protest behavior, difficulty with soothing.
- **Avoidant Attachment** — Deactivated attachment system. Self-reliance as protection. Discomfort with closeness; suppression of attachment needs.
- **Disorganized Attachment** — The attachment figure was simultaneously source of fear and comfort. No coherent strategy. Fragmented self-states.
- **Internal Working Models** — Templates for how relationships work, built in early life. Predict how others will respond and shape how we interpret their behavior.
- **Earned Security** — Security can be developed in adulthood through consistent, attuned relationships — therapeutic or personal. The past is not destiny.
- **Protest Behavior** — The activated attachment system's response to perceived abandonment: anger, pursuit, escalation.
- **Deactivating Strategies** — Avoidant patterns: minimizing need, intellectualizing emotion, distancing at moments of closeness.

## Key Figures
- **John Bowlby** — The founder. *Attachment*, *Separation*, *Loss* trilogy.
- **Mary Ainsworth** — The Strange Situation. Identified secure, anxious, and avoidant attachment in infancy.
- **Mary Main** — Adult Attachment Interview. Identified disorganized attachment and earned security.
- **Sue Johnson** — Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT). Applies attachment to couples work.

## When to Draw On This
- Recurring interpersonal patterns across multiple relationships
- Fear of abandonment or fear of engulfment
- Difficulty with closeness or difficulty with autonomy in relationships
- Protest behavior or distancing that the person can't explain or stop
- Someone whose relational style is affecting their current relationship

## Sample Language
> *"The way you respond when someone gets close — pulling back, finding reasons to doubt — that's not a character flaw. It's a strategy that made sense at some point. The question is whether it still serves you."*
