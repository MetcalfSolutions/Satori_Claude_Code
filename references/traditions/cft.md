# CFT (Compassion-Focused Therapy) — Deep Reference

## Core Concepts
- **Three Emotional Regulation Systems:**
  1. **Threat System** — Fight/flight/freeze. When chronically activated: anxiety, anger, self-criticism.
  2. **Drive System** — Seeking, wanting, achieving. Energizing but not soothing. Never-enough thinking.
  3. **Soothing System** — Safety, connection, contentment. Activated by warmth, touch, kind voice, belonging. Many people have an underdeveloped soothing system.
- **Self-Criticism as Threat-System Activation** — The inner critic activates the same neural circuits as an external attacker. You can't "think your way out" — the threat system doesn't respond to instruction.
- **Compassionate Mind Training** — Deliberately cultivating the soothing system through warmth and safety. Not a feeling to summon but a capacity to train.
- **The Compassionate Self** — A motivated orientation toward suffering with qualities: wisdom, strength, commitment, warmth, non-judgment, distress tolerance.
- **Shame as Core Organizing Emotion** — "There is something wrong with me" (not "I did something wrong"). CFT addresses shame directly.
- **Evolutionary Functional Analysis** — "It's not your fault" at the biological level. The negativity bias, self-critical voice, social comparison drive — features of the human brain, not personal failings.

## Key Figures
- **Paul Gilbert** — The founder of CFT. *The Compassionate Mind*, *Overcoming Depression*.

## When to Draw On This
- Relentless self-criticism or shame cycles
- Someone whose inner critic is their most active internal voice
- Someone who intellectually understands self-compassion but cannot access it
- Chronic anxiety and hyper-vigilance
- Someone who dismisses self-kindness as weakness or excuse-making

## Sample Language
> *"When you criticize yourself like that, your body is treating it as a genuine threat. The stress you feel afterward? That's your nervous system responding to an attack — and you're both the attacker and the target."*
> *"There's a difference between holding yourself accountable and punishing yourself. One leads somewhere. The other just keeps you in the same loop."*
