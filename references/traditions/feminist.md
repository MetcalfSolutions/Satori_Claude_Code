## Core Concepts
- **Ethics of Care (Gilligan, Noddings)** — Moral reasoning centered on relationships, context, and responsiveness. Care is not weakness — it is a sophisticated moral stance.
- **Intersectionality (Crenshaw)** — Overlapping systems of discrimination (race, class, gender, sexuality) create experiences that are not simply additive but qualitatively different.
- **Relational-Cultural Theory (Miller, Jordan)** — Human beings grow through connection, not separation. Isolation is not strength; connection is the condition for development.
- **Love as Practice (bell hooks)** — Love is not a feeling but a commitment to growth — in oneself and in others. *"Love is as love does."*
- **Anger as Information (Audre Lorde)** — Anger at injustice is legitimate, necessary, and carries information. The silencing of women's anger is itself a form of oppression.
- **The Personal Is Political** — Private struggles connect to structural conditions. Individual suffering is not only personal.
- **Performativity (Butler)** — Gender (and identity more broadly) is not a fixed essence but a performance, enacted and enforced through repetition.

## Key Figures
- **bell hooks** — *All About Love*, *The Will to Change*, *Feminism Is for Everybody*. Accessible, warm, sharp.
- **Audre Lorde** — *Sister Outsider*. On anger, self-care, and intersectionality.
- **Kimberlé Crenshaw** — The foundational theorist of intersectionality.
- **Carol Gilligan** — *In a Different Voice*. Care ethics and moral development.
- **Adrienne Maree Brown** — *Emergent Strategy*. Social change and the wisdom of natural systems.

## When to Draw On This
- Care, relationships, and the ethics of responsiveness
- Suffering that is inseparable from structural conditions
- Love as practice rather than romantic feeling
- Anger as legitimate response to injustice — not to be managed away
- When individualistic frameworks can't hold the full relational and systemic picture
