## Existentialism

- **Albert Camus** — The absurd: life has no inherent meaning, and the honest response is not despair but revolt and creation. *"One must imagine Sisyphus happy."*
- **Jean-Paul Sartre** — Radical freedom and radical responsibility. *"Existence precedes essence."*
- **Søren Kierkegaard** — Anxiety as the dizziness of freedom. The leap of faith as trust, not certainty.
- **Simone de Beauvoir** — Freedom, situation, and the ethics of genuine engagement with the other.
- **Viktor Frankl** — Logotherapy. Meaning as the primary human motivation. Can be found in work, love, and suffering itself. *Man's Search for Meaning* is essential.

## Virtue Ethics & Ancient Philosophy
- **Aristotle** — Eudaimonia (flourishing); virtue as habit cultivated by practice; the good life as activity in accordance with virtue.
- **Socratic Method** — Questions rather than answers. Aporia (productive confusion) as the beginning of wisdom.

## Other Philosophical Touchstones
- **Friedrich Nietzsche** — Amor fati; eternal recurrence as test of affirmation; creating values. Useful for strength, meaning, and self-creation.
- **Iris Murdoch** — Attention as a moral act. The sovereignty of good. Underused.
- **Blaise Pascal** — *"All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone."*

## Key Texts
- *Man's Search for Meaning* (Frankl) — Essential. Short and transformative.
- *The Myth of Sisyphus* (Camus) — On the absurd.
- *Nausea* (Sartre) — Existential confrontation in fiction.
- *Either/Or* (Kierkegaard) — On the choices that constitute a self.

## When to Draw On This
- Questions of meaning, purpose, or the felt absence of both
- Radical freedom and its weight
- Responsibility for who one is becoming
- Suffering that needs meaning-making, not consolation
- When someone needs their thinking sharpened alongside emotional support
