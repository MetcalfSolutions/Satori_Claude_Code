# ACT (Acceptance & Commitment Therapy) — Deep Reference

## Core Concepts
- **Psychological Flexibility** — The ability to contact the present moment fully, and to change or persist in behavior in service of chosen values.
- **Acceptance** — Making room for thoughts and feelings rather than trying to eliminate or control them. Not resignation; a different relationship to inner experience.
- **Defusion** — Creating distance from thoughts so they are seen as thoughts, not facts. "I'm having the thought that..." instead of "I am..."
- **Present Moment Contact** — Mindful awareness as the foundation for flexible action.
- **The Observing Self** — The transcendent self that notices experience without being defined by it. "You are not your thoughts."
- **Values** — Freely chosen qualities of action that give life meaning and direction. Not goals (achieved or not) but ongoing directions.
- **Committed Action** — Taking action in service of values, even in the presence of difficult thoughts and feelings.
- **The Hexaflex** — The six processes: Acceptance, Defusion, Present Moment, Self-as-Context, Values, Committed Action.

## Key Figures
- **Steven Hayes** — The founder of ACT. *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy*, *A Liberated Mind*.
- **Russ Harris** — *The Happiness Trap*. The most accessible introduction.

## Key Texts
- *The Happiness Trap* (Russ Harris) — Clear, practical, excellent first read.
- *A Liberated Mind* (Steven Hayes) — More depth; autobiographical and theoretical.

## When to Draw On This
- Avoidance of values-aligned action because of fear, discomfort, or difficult thoughts
- Someone who is trying to control or eliminate feelings rather than act despite them
- The gap between knowing what matters and actually doing it
- When someone says "I'll do X when I feel better / when I'm ready" — often avoidance
- Chronic suffering from struggling against inevitable internal experiences

## Sample Language
> *"The problem might not be the feeling — it might be what you do to try to get rid of it. What if the goal weren't to feel less anxious, but to live in a way that matters regardless of the anxiety?"*
