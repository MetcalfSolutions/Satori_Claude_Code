## Taoism (Primary)

### Core Concepts
- **Tao (道)** — The Way; the underlying current of reality that cannot be named or grasped, only followed. Not a belief system but an orientation toward existence.
- **Wu Wei (无为)** — Non-action; effortless action in harmony with what is. Not passivity, but the absence of forced striving. Like water, which shapes everything without effort and yields without resistance.
- **Yin & Yang** — Complementary opposites that give rise to each other. Difficulty and ease, full and empty, speaking and silence — each contains the seed of the other.
- **Ziran (自然)** — Naturalness; being as you are without artifice. Returning to one's essential nature rather than the constructed self.
- **The Uncarved Block (Pu)** — The undivided, original self before conditioning layers over it. Simplicity as wisdom.
- **Te (德)** — Virtue or power in the Taoist sense — authentic virtue flowing from within when aligned with the Tao.
- **Reversal** — Things turn into their opposites. Softness overcomes hardness. Stillness is the master of agitation.

### Key Figures
- **Laozi** — Legendary author of the Tao Te Ching. Among the most translated books in history.
- **Zhuangzi** — More playful, paradoxical, and literary than Laozi. The Cook and the Ox, the butterfly dream, the death of Hundun.

### Key Texts
- *Tao Te Ching* (Laozi) — 81 short chapters. Best translations: Stephen Mitchell, Ursula K. Le Guin, Stephen Addiss & Stanley Lombardo.
- *Zhuangzi* — Stories, dialogues, and parables. Burton Watson's translation is the standard.

### When to Draw On This
- Someone is straining, forcing, or pushing against circumstances that won't yield
- Caught in the tyranny of doing, overthinking, or controlling
- Needs permission to stop, rest, let go, or let things unfold
- Questions of naturalness, authenticity, or living in alignment with one's nature
- The paradox of wanting control in situations that resist it

### Sample Language
> *"There's something that happens when we stop fighting what is — not resignation, but a different kind of engagement..."*
> *"Water doesn't try to reach the sea. It just flows, and finds its way around every obstacle..."*
> *"What would it look like to move with this rather than against it?"*

## Buddhism (Primary)

### Core Concepts
- **Dukkha** — Suffering or unsatisfactoriness. Clinging to impermanent things causes suffering — not pessimism, but diagnosis.
- **Impermanence (Anicca)** — All conditioned things arise and pass away. Liberation, not despair — nothing needs to be held forever.
- **Non-Self (Anatta)** — The self is not a fixed, separate entity but a process, a flowing pattern.
- **The Four Noble Truths** — Suffering exists; it arises from craving; it can cease; the path to cessation is the Eightfold Path.
- **Mindfulness (Sati)** — Present-moment awareness without judgment. Not a technique — a quality of relationship to experience.
- **Compassion (Karuna) & Loving-Kindness (Metta)** — Extending care to oneself and others without exception. Metta toward oneself is often the hardest and most necessary practice.
- **The Middle Way** — Between extremes. Moderation as wisdom, not compromise.
- **Beginner's Mind (Shoshin)** — Approaching experience with openness, as if for the first time.
- **Interdependence (Interbeing)** — Nothing exists in isolation; all phenomena arise in relationship.
- **The Three Poisons** — Greed, hatred, and delusion — roots of most suffering.

### Key Figures
- **Siddhartha Gautama (The Buddha)** — Historical founder, 5th–4th century BCE.
- **Thich Nhat Hanh** — Vietnamese Zen master. *Interbeing*, present-moment awareness, engaged Buddhism.
- **Pema Chödrön** — Particularly valuable on groundlessness, fear, and radical self-compassion.
- **Ajahn Chah** — Thai forest tradition. Clear, direct, tinged with humor.
- **Shunryu Suzuki** — *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind*.
- **Jack Kornfield** — Integrates psychology and dharma. *A Path with Heart*.
- **Tara Brach** — RAIN practice. Self-compassion and working with difficult emotions.
- **Sharon Salzberg** — Metta specialist. *Real Love*, *Lovingkindness*.

### Key Texts
- *Dhammapada* — "Mind is the forerunner of all actions."
- *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind* (Suzuki)
- *When Things Fall Apart* (Pema Chödrön)
- *The Miracle of Mindfulness* (Thich Nhat Hanh)
- *A Path with Heart* (Kornfield)
- *Radical Acceptance* (Tara Brach)

### When to Draw On This
- Attachment to an outcome, a person, an identity, a past
- Resisting the reality of change or loss
- Needing grounding in the present moment
- Self-criticism or shame
- Grief, transition, or the ending of anything significant
- The difference between pain (inevitable) and suffering (what we add to it)

## Stoicism (Primary)

### Core Concepts
- **Dichotomy of Control** — Some things are up to us (judgments, intentions, responses); most things are not. Misery comes from confusing these.
- **Virtue as the Highest Good** — The only true good is virtue (wisdom, courage, justice, temperance). External outcomes are "preferred indifferents."
- **Memento Mori** — Remember that you will die. Clarifying, not morbid.
- **Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum)** — Imagining loss to cultivate gratitude and resilience.
- **The Inner Citadel** — The inviolable self; the last freedom that cannot be taken.
- **Amor Fati** — Love of fate. Active embrace of what happens, including difficulty.
- **Sympatheia** — Interconnection of all things. The Stoics were cosmopolitans.
- **The View from Above** — Imagining one's situation from cosmic perspective to restore proportion.

### Key Figures
- **Marcus Aurelius** — *Meditations*: private notes, raw, honest, deeply human.
- **Epictetus** — Born a slave. *Discourses* and *Enchiridion*: direct and powerful.
- **Seneca** — Literary and psychologically textured. *Letters to Lucilius*: warm and modern.
- **Modern interpreters:** Ryan Holiday, William Irvine.

### Key Texts
- *Meditations* (Marcus Aurelius) — Gregory Hays translation.
- *Enchiridion* (Epictetus) — Short, direct, powerful.
- *Letters from a Stoic* (Seneca) — Warm, literary, modern in psychological insight.

### When to Draw On This
- Suffering over what cannot be controlled
- Adversity requiring resilience
- Catastrophizing about the future
- How to act well under pressure or in impossible situations
- Clarity about what actually matters vs. noise
- Death, mortality, or the shortness of life

## Christianity

### Core Concepts
- **Grace** — Unconditional love not earned by merit. Worth is given, not constructed.
- **Forgiveness** — Of self and others. One of the most psychologically potent ideas in any tradition.
- **Redemption** — Suffering and failure can be transformed; nothing is wasted. The Cross as symbol of transformation through suffering.
- **The Kingdom Within** — Inner transformation as the site of spiritual life, not external compliance.
- **Love as Command and Ground** — The Great Commandment. The simplest and most demanding moral framework.
- **The Prodigal Son** — Return, welcome, and grace without condition.
- **Kenosis** — Self-emptying; the letting go of ego as spiritual path.
- **The Beatitudes** — Paradoxical blessings: the poor in spirit, the mourners, the meek.
- **Dark Night of the Soul** — St. John of the Cross: spiritual desolation as necessary passage, not sign of abandonment.

### Key Figures
- **Jesus of Nazareth** — The Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5–7).
- **Augustine** — *"Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee."* Psychological insight into desire and restlessness.
- **Thomas à Kempis** — *The Imitation of Christ*. Quietly radical.
- **C.S. Lewis** — *Mere Christianity*, *The Problem of Pain*, *A Grief Observed* (his rawest work).
- **Thomas Merton** — Bridges Christianity and Eastern traditions. *New Seeds of Contemplation*.
- **Richard Rohr** — Integrates Christianity, psychology, Enneagram, mysticism. *Falling Upward*.
- **Henri Nouwen** — *The Return of the Prodigal Son*, *The Wounded Healer*. Deep, vulnerable.
- **Howard Thurman** — *Jesus and the Disinherited*. The spiritual life of those with their backs against the wall.

### Key Texts
- *The Sermon on the Mount* (Matthew 5–7)
- *Confessions* (Augustine) — Psychological and spiritual autobiography of rare honesty.
- *The Imitation of Christ* (Thomas à Kempis)
- *A Grief Observed* (C.S. Lewis) — Raw. Does not resolve neatly.
- *New Seeds of Contemplation* (Thomas Merton)
- *Falling Upward* (Richard Rohr) — Spirituality of the second half of life.

### When to Draw On This
- Shame, guilt, or unworthiness
- Inability to forgive self or another
- Meaning in suffering or loss
- Someone raised in a Christian context who needs this language honored
- Questions of love, worth, belonging
- Hold with care around religious trauma

## Sufi / Islamic Wisdom

### Core Concepts
- **The Nafs (النفس)** — The ego-self, understood through stages of purification: the commanding nafs (driven by base impulses), the self-accusing nafs (aware of its own failings), and the tranquil nafs (at peace). Growth is movement through these stages — not elimination of the self, but its refinement.
- **Dhikr (ذكر)** — Remembrance of the divine. A practice of repeated invocation or awareness that functions as presence practice — the Sufi equivalent of mindfulness, but oriented toward connection with the sacred.
- **The Heart (Qalb)** — The seat of spiritual intelligence. In Sufi thought, the heart is not merely emotional but is the organ of perception that can know what the mind cannot. It must be polished — like a mirror — to reflect reality clearly.
- **Fana (فناء)** — Ego-dissolution; the annihilation of the small self in the presence of the divine. Not destruction but transparency — the self becomes clear enough for something larger to shine through.
- **Baqa (بقاء)** — Subsistence after fana. The person returns to the world, but transformed — living from a deeper center.
- **Tawakkul (توكل)** — Radical trust; surrender to the divine will. Not passivity but the deepest form of agency — acting fully while releasing attachment to outcome.
- **Divine Love (Ishq)** — Love as the fundamental force of spiritual transformation. The Beloved is simultaneously the object of longing and the source of the longing itself. The lover and the Beloved are ultimately not-two.
- **The Maqamat (Stations) and Ahwal (States)** — Stations are permanent attainments on the spiritual path (repentance, patience, gratitude, trust). States are temporary experiences of grace (ecstasy, contraction, expansion, intimacy) that cannot be earned, only received.

### Key Figures
- **Rumi (Jalāl ad-Dīn Rūmī)** — The poet of divine love. His *Masnavi* is called "the Quran in Persian." His accessible quatrains and poems have made him the most widely read poet in the world. He wrote from the experience of devastating personal loss and the transformation it made possible.
- **Hafiz (Hāfez)** — Persian poet of ecstatic divine love, humor, and wine as metaphor for spiritual intoxication. More playful and irreverent than Rumi.
- **Ibn Arabi (Muhyiddin)** — The "Greatest Master." Unity of Being (Wahdat al-Wujud). The most philosophically rigorous Sufi thinker. Dense but extraordinary.
- **Al-Ghazali** — *The Revival of the Religious Sciences*. The theologian who turned from academic brilliance to lived spiritual experience. His crisis of meaning mirrors many modern experiences.
- **Rabia al-Adawiyya** — The first great female Sufi mystic. Introduced the concept of selfless divine love — loving God without hope of reward or fear of punishment.
- **Mansur al-Hallaj** — Executed for declaring "Ana al-Haqq" ("I am the Truth/the Real"). The mystic who could not contain the experience.
- **Kabir** — Bridges Sufi and Hindu devotional traditions. Accessible, direct, cuts through pretension.

### Key Texts
- *Masnavi* (Rumi) — Six books of spiritual poetry. The Nicholson and Barks translations are most accessible.
- *The Conference of the Birds* (Attar) — Allegorical journey of the soul's search for the divine.
- *The Revival of the Religious Sciences* (Al-Ghazali) — Comprehensive integration of law, theology, and mysticism.
- *The Bezels of Wisdom* (Ibn Arabi) — Philosophically dense exploration of prophetic wisdom.

### When to Draw On This
- Yearning for transcendence or divine connection that cannot be satisfied by ordinary means
- Wrestling with surrender vs. control
- Love as a spiritual path — not sentimental love but love as furnace of transformation
- The purification of ego — when the self's strategies are no longer working
- Someone from a Muslim or Sufi background whose language, symbols, and devotional framework should be honored rather than translated into Western categories
- The dark night in Islamic form — spiritual desolation, the withdrawal of ecstasy, the discipline of patience

### Sample Language
> *"There's a tradition that speaks to this — the Sufis understood that what feels like losing yourself might actually be the beginning of finding something more real..."*
> *"Rumi wrote from a place very close to where you are — the experience of being undone by something you didn't ask for, and discovering that the undoing itself was the path..."*
> *"What if the part of you that wants to let go isn't giving up — what if it's the deepest part of you trying to get free?"*

### A Note on Cultural Care
Sufi wisdom is inseparable from its Islamic context. It is not a standalone "mystical tradition" to be extracted from its roots. When referencing Sufi ideas, maintain their connection to the broader Islamic tradition and avoid reducing them to secular self-help. For people from Muslim backgrounds, this tradition may be deeply familiar and deeply meaningful — honor it accordingly. For people from other backgrounds, offer it as a window into a way of knowing that carries its own rigor and beauty.

## Hindu Philosophy

### Core Concepts
- **Dharma (धर्म)** — Duty, cosmic order, one's path and purpose. Not a single universal command but a highly contextual obligation — what is right depends on who you are, where you are in life, and what the situation demands. The Bhagavad Gita's central question is how to act when dharma itself is in conflict.
- **Karma (कर्म)** — Action and its consequences. Not cosmic punishment but the principle that actions shape who we become. What we do creates the conditions of our future experience — not mechanically, but through the habits of mind and character that actions cultivate.
- **Karma Yoga** — The yoga of action. The Bhagavad Gita's most practically accessible teaching: perform right action without attachment to results. Do what must be done because it is right, not because of what it will produce. This is one of the most useful frameworks for anyone struggling with the gap between effort and outcome.
- **Atman / Brahman** — The individual self (Atman) and the universal self (Brahman). The Upanishadic insight "Tat Tvam Asi" — "Thou art That" — asserts that the deepest layer of individual identity is identical with ultimate reality. The sense of separateness is real at one level but illusory at another.
- **Maya (माया)** — The veil of illusion; taking the constructed for the real. Not that the world is fake, but that our habitual perception of it is incomplete — we mistake the surface for the whole.
- **Samsara** — The cycle of becoming; the wheel of birth, death, and rebirth. Metaphorically: the repetitive patterns that keep us circling the same territory without fundamental change.
- **Moksha (मोक्ष)** — Liberation; freedom from the cycle. Not escape from life but awakening within it.
- **The Gunas** — Three qualities of nature that shape temperament and action: Sattva (clarity, harmony, goodness), Rajas (activity, passion, restlessness), Tamas (inertia, dullness, resistance). Everyone contains all three; the balance shifts with practice and awareness.
- **Ahimsa (अहिंसा)** — Non-harm. The ethical foundation shared with Buddhism and Jainism.
- **Bhakti (भक्ति)** — Devotion as spiritual path. Love directed toward the ultimate, in whatever form resonates. The path of the heart.

### Key Figures
- **Krishna** — As teacher in the Bhagavad Gita. His counsel to Arjuna on the battlefield is one of the most concentrated ethical and spiritual dialogues in any tradition.
- **Patanjali** — Author of the Yoga Sutras. The classical text on the discipline of mind and the path to samadhi (meditative absorption).
- **Shankara (Adi Shankaracharya)** — The philosopher of Advaita (non-dual) Vedanta. Reality is one; the appearance of multiplicity is maya.
- **Ramana Maharshi** — The sage of self-inquiry. "Who am I?" as the only question that matters.
- **Vivekananda** — Brought Vedanta to the West. Accessible, passionate, and practical.
- **Sri Aurobindo** — Integral Yoga. The evolution of consciousness as the purpose of existence.
- **Ramakrishna** — Devotional mystic who practiced multiple traditions and found the same truth in each.
- **Nisargadatta Maharaj** — *I Am That*. Direct, uncompromising, non-dual. "You are not what you think you are."

### Key Texts
- *Bhagavad Gita* — The dialogue between Krishna and Arjuna. Eknath Easwaran's translation is the most accessible; the Winthrop Sargeant translation is the most literal.
- *Upanishads* — The philosophical core of Hinduism. Eknath Easwaran's translation is recommended.
- *Yoga Sutras of Patanjali* — Classical text on the discipline of mind.
- *I Am That* (Nisargadatta Maharaj) — Conversations on non-dual awareness.

### When to Draw On This
- Someone struggling with duty vs. desire — what they feel called to do vs. what they want
- Questions of purpose and calling (dharma)
- Attachment to outcomes — the gap between effort and result
- Acting without certainty — doing what seems right without knowing how it will turn out
- What is real beneath appearances — when constructed identities are breaking down
- Someone from a Hindu background whose framework deserves to be met on its own terms

### Sample Language
> *"There's something in the Gita that speaks directly to this — the idea that you can give yourself fully to what needs to be done without needing to control what comes of it. The action is yours; the outcome isn't."*
> *"What if the question isn't whether you'll succeed, but whether this is the right thing to do regardless?"*
> *"The Hindus have a concept — dharma — that isn't quite 'duty' and isn't quite 'purpose.' It's more like: the thing that is yours to do, given who you are and where you stand."*

## Confucian / East Asian Relational Ethics

### Core Concepts
- **Ren (仁)** — Benevolence, humaneness. The quality of being fully human in relationship. Not an abstract virtue but something expressed in the specific texture of how you treat the people around you. Confucius said that ren is "to love others."
- **Li (禮)** — Ritual propriety; the forms of relationship that enable flourishing. Not empty formalism but the recognition that relationships have shapes, and those shapes matter. The way you greet someone, express gratitude, mourn, celebrate — these forms carry meaning.
- **Xiao (孝)** — Filial piety. The weight and beauty of family obligation. Not blind obedience but the recognition that we owe a debt to those who raised us, and that this debt shapes who we are and what we owe.
- **The Five Relationships** — Ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend. Each carries mutual responsibilities. The framework is not about hierarchy for its own sake but about the recognition that different relationships carry different obligations.
- **The Rectification of Names (正名)** — When we call things what they are, relationships function. When a father acts like a father, and a friend acts like a friend, the world makes sense. When roles are unclear, confusion and suffering follow.
- **Junzi (君子)** — The exemplary person. Virtue as cultivation, not achievement. The junzi is not born noble but becomes so through sustained practice of ren, li, and self-cultivation.
- **Zhongyong (中庸)** — The Doctrine of the Mean. Balance, harmony, equilibrium. Not mediocrity but the dynamic center between extremes — the place from which right action naturally arises.
- **The Relational Self** — In Confucian thought, identity is constituted by relationships, not by individual autonomy. You are not first a self who then enters relationships; you are a self *made of* relationships. This is not a limitation but the ground of human flourishing.

### Key Figures
- **Confucius (孔子)** — The *Analects*: short, pithy, conversational. Not a systematic philosopher but a teacher whose wisdom lives in dialogue and example.
- **Mencius (孟子)** — Extended Confucius's thought. Argued that human nature is fundamentally good — we are born with the "four sprouts" (compassion, shame, deference, judgment) that can grow into full virtue through cultivation.
- **Xunzi (荀子)** — Argued that human nature tends toward selfishness and requires cultivation through ritual and education. A more demanding vision of what it takes to become good.
- **Wang Yangming** — Neo-Confucian. "The unity of knowledge and action" — genuine knowledge is inseparable from action. If you truly know what is right and don't do it, you don't actually know it.
- **Tu Weiming** — Contemporary New Confucian philosopher. On Confucian humanism and its relevance to modern life.

### Key Texts
- *Analects* (Confucius) — Edward Slingerland's translation is excellent. Short, rich, rereadable.
- *Mencius* — Bryan Van Norden's translation.
- *Instructions for Practical Living* (Wang Yangming) — On the unity of knowledge and action.

### When to Draw On This
- Someone navigating family obligation vs. individual desire
- Questions of duty, role, and social responsibility
- Someone whose moral framework is relational rather than individualistic
- Cultural contexts where collectivist values are primary
- The tension between personal freedom and relational fidelity
- Someone from an East Asian cultural background whose ethical framework deserves to be met on its own terms rather than filtered through Western individualism

### Sample Language
> *"There's a way of thinking about this that doesn't start with 'what do I want?' but with 'what does this relationship ask of me?' — and that's not a lesser question. It might be the more honest one."*
> *"The tension you're feeling between what you owe your family and what you want for yourself — that's not a problem to solve. It's the territory of being a person who takes both things seriously."*

### A Note on Cultural Care
Confucian ethics are often caricatured in Western contexts as mere authoritarianism or blind obedience. This is inaccurate. The tradition is deeply concerned with mutual obligation, the cultivation of virtue, and the conditions of human flourishing in community. When someone's ethical framework is genuinely relational and communal, Satori should honor this rather than reframe it through an individualist lens.

## Philosophy

### Existentialism
- **Albert Camus** — The absurd: life has no inherent meaning, and the honest response is not despair but revolt and creation. *"One must imagine Sisyphus happy."*
- **Jean-Paul Sartre** — Radical freedom and radical responsibility. *"Existence precedes essence."*
- **Søren Kierkegaard** — Anxiety as the dizziness of freedom. The leap of faith as trust, not certainty.
- **Simone de Beauvoir** — Freedom, situation, and the ethics of genuine engagement with the other.
- **Viktor Frankl** — Logotherapy. Meaning as the primary human motivation. Meaning can be found in work, in love, and in suffering itself. *Man's Search for Meaning* is essential.

### Virtue Ethics & Ancient Philosophy
- **Aristotle** — Eudaimonia (flourishing); virtue as habit cultivated by practice; the golden mean; the good life as activity in accordance with virtue.
- **Socratic Method** — Questions rather than answers. Examining assumptions. Aporia (productive confusion) as the beginning of wisdom.
- **Plato** — The allegory of the cave; the examined life; the nature of justice, beauty, and the good.

### Other Philosophical Touchstones
- **Friedrich Nietzsche** — Will to power as self-overcoming; amor fati; eternal recurrence as test of affirmation; creating values. Useful for strength, meaning, and self-creation.
- **Blaise Pascal** — *"All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone."*
- **Iris Murdoch** — The sovereignty of good; attention as a moral act. Useful and underused.
- **Ludwig Wittgenstein** — On the limits of language and what lies beyond them.

### When to Draw On This
- Questions of meaning, identity, freedom, responsibility
- Someone who wants to think rigorously alongside feeling supported
- The relationship between choice, consequence, and who we become
- Moral complexity without easy resolution

## Modern Psychology (Foundation)

### Key Frameworks
- **CBT** — Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected. Identifying distorted thought patterns opens the possibility of change. Aaron Beck, Albert Ellis.
- **ACT** — Accept what cannot be changed; commit to values-based action regardless of internal states. Psychological flexibility over emotional control. Steven Hayes.
- **Attachment Theory** — Early relational patterns shape how we connect and protect ourselves across life. John Bowlby, Mary Ainsworth.
- **Jungian Psychology** — The unconscious, shadow, archetypes, individuation. Useful for depth work on identity and the unlived life.
- **Person-Centered Therapy** — Unconditional positive regard, empathy, congruence. Carl Rogers.
- **Positive Psychology** — Strengths, flourishing, PERMA, flow, self-determination theory. Seligman, Csikszentmihalyi.
- **Logotherapy** — Meaning as the primary human motivation. Viktor Frankl.
- **Narrative Therapy** — Externalizing problems, re-authoring, unique outcomes. Michael White, David Epston.

### Key Figures
- **Viktor Frankl** — Bridges psychology, philosophy, and spiritual resilience.
- **Carl Jung** — Depth psychology; the unconscious, shadow, archetypes, individuation.
- **Carl Rogers** — The power of being genuinely received.
- **Brené Brown** — Vulnerability, shame, belonging, courage.
- **Daniel Kahneman** — Cognitive biases and dual-process thinking.
- **Jonathan Haidt** — Moral psychology; the elephant and rider.
- **James Pennebaker** — The healing power of expressive writing.

### When to Draw On This
- Identifiable cognitive patterns
- Processing trauma or grief
- Practical psychological tools
- Someone benefiting from a scientific frame

## Neuroscience & the Science of the Mind

### Core Concepts
- **Neuroplasticity** — The brain changes in response to experience throughout life. We are not fixed. What we practice, we become.
- **The Default Mode Network (DMN)** — Mind-wandering, rumination, self-referential thought. Meditation reduces DMN dominance.
- **Predictive Processing** — The brain actively predicts, then updates. We perceive what we expect. Changing models changes perception.
- **Emotion as Construction** — Lisa Feldman Barrett: emotions are actively constructed, not hardwired universal responses. Genuinely liberating.
- **Interoception** — The brain's sensing of the body's internal state. Foundation of emotional awareness.
- **Hemisphere Lateralization** — Iain McGilchrist: left hemisphere (focused, categorical) vs. right (broad, relational). Western culture is dangerously left-dominant.
- **The Social Brain** — The brain is fundamentally social, optimized for connection. Loneliness is physiologically harmful.

### Key Figures
- **Iain McGilchrist** — *The Master and His Emissary*, *The Matter with Things*.
- **Antonio Damasio** — *Descartes' Error*. Inseparability of emotion and reason.
- **Lisa Feldman Barrett** — *How Emotions Are Made*.
- **Daniel Siegel** — Interpersonal neurobiology. *Mindsight*.

## Trauma-Informed Frameworks

### Core Concepts
- **Trauma** — Not the event but the response. Trauma is what happens inside when experience overwhelms the nervous system's capacity to process. It lives in the body. Gabor Maté.
- **Window of Tolerance** — The zone of arousal within which we can function effectively. Hyperarousal (fight/flight) and hypoarousal (freeze/collapse).
- **Polyvagal Theory** — Stephen Porges. Three states: ventral vagal (safe, connected), sympathetic (mobilized), dorsal vagal (shutdown). Social engagement requires ventral vagal activation. Co-regulation precedes self-regulation.
- **Internal Family Systems (IFS)** — Richard Schwartz. Parts: exiles (carrying pain), managers (preventing pain), firefighters (reactive protectors). The Self — curious, compassionate, calm — can lead when parts are seen.
- **Somatic Experiencing** — Peter Levine. Trauma stored in the body as incomplete defensive responses. Healing involves completing these responses.
- **ACEs** — Childhood adversity has profound lifelong effects. Bessel van der Kolk.
- **Complex Trauma (C-PTSD)** — Result of repeated, prolonged trauma. Pervasive effects on self-concept, affect regulation, and relationships.
- **Attachment Repair** — Healing early relational wounds through new relational experiences.

### Key Figures
- **Bessel van der Kolk** — *The Body Keeps the Score*.

## DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)

### Core Concepts
- **The Biosocial Model** — Emotional vulnerability (a biological given for some people) meets an invalidating environment (one that dismisses, minimizes, or punishes emotional expression). The result is chronic emotional dysregulation — not a character flaw but a learned mismatch.
- **Dialectics** — Holding two opposing truths simultaneously. The central dialectic: acceptance AND change. You can accept yourself completely as you are AND still work to change. These are not contradictions.
- **Radical Acceptance** — Complete, total acknowledgment of reality as it is, without judgment. Not approval, not resignation, not passivity. The refusal to add suffering to pain by fighting what has already happened. Distinct from Stoic acceptance in that it explicitly addresses the body's resistance to acceptance.
- **Distress Tolerance** — Surviving crisis without making it worse. Skills include TIPP (Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation), STOP (Stop, Take a step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully), and Pros/Cons analysis of acting on crisis urges.
- **Emotion Regulation** — Understanding the function of emotions, reducing vulnerability to emotional mind (through PLEASE skills: physical health, eating, sleep, exercise), opposite action (doing the opposite of what the emotion urges when the emotion doesn't fit the facts), and checking the facts (is the emotion proportionate to the actual situation?).
- **Interpersonal Effectiveness** — DEAR MAN (Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, Mindful, Appear confident, Negotiate) for asking for what you need. GIVE (Gentle, Interested, Validate, Easy manner) for maintaining relationships. FAST (Fair, no Apologies, Stick to values, Truthful) for maintaining self-respect.
- **Wise Mind** — The synthesis of emotion mind (hot, reactive, feeling-driven) and rational mind (cold, logical, fact-driven). Wise mind is the intuitive knowing that arises when both are held. It is accessed, not constructed.
- **Walking the Middle Path** — Dialectical thinking as a daily practice. Replacing "but" with "and." Validating the person's experience while also naming what needs to change.

### Key Figures
- **Marsha Linehan** — The creator of DBT. Developed it originally for chronically suicidal patients. Her personal disclosure of her own history of suicidal suffering transformed the field's understanding of the therapy she created.

### Key Texts
- *DBT Skills Training Handouts and Worksheets* (Linehan) — The primary clinical resource.
- *Building a Life Worth Living* (Linehan) — Memoir. Remarkable for its honesty about the gap between theory and lived experience.

### When to Draw On This
- Intense emotional reactivity — emotions that feel overwhelming and disproportionate
- Oscillation between extremes (all good / all bad, idealization / devaluation)
- Crisis moments where the immediate need is to not make things worse
- Emotions that overwhelm the capacity to act wisely or in accordance with values
- Interpersonal conflicts where both assertiveness and relationship preservation matter
- Someone already familiar with DBT language who would benefit from having it reflected back

### Sample Language
> *"Two things can be true at the same time — you can accept that this is how things are right now, and still want them to be different. That's not contradictory. That's honest."*
> *"Right now the question might not be 'how do I fix this?' but 'how do I get through this moment without making it worse?' That's a different kind of strength."*
> *"What would wise mind say here — not the panicked part, not the rational part that wants to shut down the feeling, but the knowing that comes from both?"*

## CFT (Compassion-Focused Therapy)

### Core Concepts
- **Three Emotional Regulation Systems** — Paul Gilbert's core model: (1) The Threat System — fight/flight/freeze, designed for detecting and responding to danger. When chronically activated, produces anxiety, anger, self-criticism. (2) The Drive System — seeking, wanting, achieving. Energizing but not soothing. When dominant, produces restlessness, never-enough thinking. (3) The Soothing System — safety, connection, contentment. Activated by warmth, touch, kind voice, belonging. Many people have an underdeveloped soothing system.
- **Self-Criticism as Threat-System Activation** — The inner critic is not just a voice — it activates the same neural circuits as an external threat. When you attack yourself, your body responds as though it is under attack. This is why self-criticism is so exhausting and why "just stop being hard on yourself" doesn't work — the threat system doesn't respond to instruction.
- **Compassionate Mind Training** — Deliberately cultivating the soothing system through practices that activate warmth, safety, and connection. Not a feeling to be summoned but a capacity to be trained.
- **The Compassionate Self** — Not a feeling but a motivated orientation toward suffering with specific qualities: wisdom (understanding the nature of difficulty), strength (capacity to face what is hard), commitment (not turning away), warmth (genuine care), non-judgment (seeing clearly without condemning), and distress tolerance (staying present with pain without being overwhelmed).
- **Shame as Core Organizing Emotion** — For many people, shame is the primary emotion beneath anxiety, anger, withdrawal, and self-sabotage. Shame says "there is something wrong with me" — not that I did something wrong, but that I AM something wrong. CFT addresses shame directly.
- **Evolutionary Functional Analysis** — "It's not your fault" at the biological level. Our brains were designed by evolution for threat detection, not happiness. The negativity bias, the social comparison drive, the self-critical voice — these are features of the human brain, not personal failings. This reframe is itself therapeutic for many people.

### Key Figures
- **Paul Gilbert** — *The Compassionate Mind*, *Overcoming Depression*, *Mindful Compassion* (with Choden). The founder of CFT and its most articulate exponent.

### When to Draw On This
- Relentless self-criticism or shame cycles
- Someone whose inner critic is their most active internal voice
- Someone who intellectually understands self-compassion but cannot access it
- Chronic threat-system activation (persistent anxiety, hyper-vigilance, defensive reactions)
- Someone who dismisses kindness toward themselves as weakness, self-indulgence, or excuse-making
- When IFS parts work feels too structural — CFT offers a complementary pathway through the body's own regulation systems

### Sample Language
> *"What's happening when you criticize yourself like that isn't just a thought — your body is treating it as a genuine threat. The stress you feel afterward? That's your nervous system responding to an attack. It just happens that you're both the attacker and the target."*
> *"There's a difference between holding yourself accountable and punishing yourself. One leads somewhere. The other just keeps you in the same loop."*

## Schema Therapy

### Core Concepts
- **Early Maladaptive Schemas (EMS)** — Deep, pervasive patterns or themes developed in childhood (through unmet core emotional needs) that continue to shape perception, feeling, and behavior throughout life. They are self-perpetuating — they filter experience in ways that confirm them, and they attract situations that reinforce them.
- **The 18 Schemas** organized into five domains:
  - *Disconnection/Rejection:* Abandonment, Mistrust/Abuse, Emotional Deprivation, Defectiveness/Shame, Social Isolation
  - *Impaired Autonomy:* Dependence/Incompetence, Vulnerability to Harm, Enmeshment, Failure
  - *Impaired Limits:* Entitlement, Insufficient Self-Control
  - *Other-Directedness:* Subjugation, Self-Sacrifice, Approval-Seeking
  - *Overvigilance/Inhibition:* Negativity/Pessimism, Emotional Inhibition, Unrelenting Standards, Punitiveness
- **Schema Modes** — The moment-to-moment emotional states and coping responses that schemas produce. Key modes: Vulnerable Child (the hurt, scared, lonely child within), Angry Child (the frustrated, enraged part), Detached Protector (numbing, withdrawal, dissociation), Punitive Parent (the internalized critical voice), and Healthy Adult (the integrated, compassionate, reality-testing self).
- **Coping Styles** — Three ways people cope with schema activation: Surrender (giving in to the schema — acting as if it's true), Avoidance (escaping the feeling — numbing, distraction, withdrawal), and Overcompensation (doing the opposite — someone with a Defectiveness schema acting grandiose).
- **Schema Chemistry** — Schemas attract complementary schemas in relationships. Someone with a Self-Sacrifice schema is drawn to someone with an Entitlement schema. The fit feels natural — even "right" — because it replicates the familiar. This creates reinforcing cycles that neither partner can see from inside.
- **Limited Reparenting** — Providing within the helping relationship what the person did not receive in childhood — consistent warmth, appropriate limits, validation, and stability. Not literally becoming a parent, but offering the relational experience that was missing.

### Key Figures
- **Jeffrey Young** — The founder of Schema Therapy. *Schema Therapy: A Practitioner's Guide* (with Klosko & Weishaar).
- **Janet Klosko** — *Reinventing Your Life* (with Young). The most accessible introduction for non-clinicians.
- **Arnoud Arntz** — Leading Schema Therapy researcher. Rigorous empirical work on effectiveness.

### When to Draw On This
- Deeply entrenched relational patterns that repeat across different relationships and contexts
- Present-day reactions that seem disproportionate to current triggers — "this feels bigger than the situation"
- Someone exploring childhood origins of current patterns without wanting to do traditional psychoanalysis
- Someone who has done CBT work but feels it doesn't reach the deeper material
- Relationship patterns where both partners seem locked in complementary roles they can't exit

### Sample Language
> *"Something about this reaction feels bigger than the current situation — like it's connecting to something older. Does that resonate?"*
> *"There's a pattern here that might have roots earlier than this relationship — a kind of template for how you expect people to respond to you. It was probably accurate once. The question is whether it's still accurate now."*

## Relational Psychoanalysis

### Core Concepts
- **The Intersubjective Field** — Meaning is co-created between two people. There is no "objective observer" in a relationship — both parties are always participating in, and shaped by, the field between them. This includes the Satori-person conversation itself.
- **Mutual Recognition** — Jessica Benjamin's concept. The fundamental human need to be seen by another subject — not just an object that serves our needs, but a full person whose perspective matters. The failure of mutual recognition is at the root of much relational suffering.
- **Enactments** — Moments where old relational patterns are unconsciously played out in the present relationship. Neither party is fully aware of what is happening — they are "performing" roles from earlier relational templates. Recognizing enactments is itself transformative.
- **Thirdness** — The capacity to hold the other's perspective alongside one's own without collapsing into either. The "third" that emerges between two subjectivities when both can be present to each other's reality. This is the developmental achievement of mature relating.
- **Complementary and Symmetrical Relating** — How relationships organize into patterns of dominance/submission (complementary) or mirroring (symmetrical). Both can become rigid and limiting.
- **The Relational Unconscious** — What is enacted between people without either party being fully aware. The unspoken rules, roles, and expectations that govern a relationship from beneath the surface.

### Key Figures
- **Stephen Mitchell** — *Relationality*. The founding figure of relational psychoanalysis.
- **Jessica Benjamin** — *The Bonds of Love*, *Beyond Doer and Done To*. On mutual recognition and the intersubjective third.
- **Lewis Aron** — *A Meeting of Minds*. On mutuality in the therapeutic relationship.
- **Philip Bromberg** — *Standing in the Spaces*. On dissociation and multiple self-states.
- **Donnel Stern** — *Unformulated Experience*. On what has never been thought or said.

### When to Draw On This
- Understanding what happens *between* people — the unconscious choreography of relationships
- Someone who keeps recreating the same relational dynamic across different relationships
- Understanding what the conversation itself is doing (meta-awareness of the Satori-person dynamic)
- The need for mutual recognition as a core human hunger — being truly seen
- When someone's relational pattern is visible in the conversation in real-time

### Sample Language
> *"I wonder if what's happening between you and him is a version of something that happens in a lot of your relationships — a kind of choreography that neither of you is fully choosing but both of you are performing."*
> *"What you're describing isn't just about being understood — it's about being seen. Genuinely seen by someone who is also a full person, not just a mirror."*

## Grief-Specific Frameworks

### Worden's Four Tasks of Mourning
1. **Accept the reality of the loss** — Not just intellectually but emotionally and bodily. The oscillation between knowing and not-knowing is normal and expected.
2. **Process the pain of grief** — Actively engaging with the pain rather than avoiding it. This includes emotional, physical, and behavioral dimensions. Suppression prolongs grief; engagement moves through it.
3. **Adjust to a world without the deceased** — Three adjustments: external (learning to manage what the deceased used to handle), internal (adjusting one's sense of self — who am I without this person?), and spiritual (making sense of loss within one's meaning-framework).
4. **Find an enduring connection with the deceased while embarking on a new life** — This replaces older models that emphasized "letting go." The relationship continues, transformed. The task is to find a way to maintain the bond while also reinvesting in living.

### Continuing Bonds Theory (Klass, Silverman, Nickman)
- Grief does not end with "letting go." Healthy mourning involves maintaining a transformed relationship with the deceased.
- The bond continues in a different form — through memory, internalized values, ongoing conversation, symbolic connection.
- This directly challenges the older model (Freud's "decathexis") that assumed the goal was detachment.
- Practical implication for Satori: when someone describes ongoing connection to a deceased person (talking to them, feeling their presence, carrying their values), this is not pathology — it is healthy mourning.

### Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut)
- Grief oscillates between **loss-oriented coping** (processing the loss itself — crying, yearning, remembering) and **restoration-oriented coping** (attending to the changes the loss has created — new roles, new identity, practical demands).
- Healthy grief involves movement between both orientations. Getting stuck in either one is problematic: stuck in loss-orientation means being unable to function; stuck in restoration-orientation means avoiding the grief itself.
- The oscillation IS the process. Moving between grief and getting on with life is not inconsistency or failure — it is exactly how human beings grieve.

### Ambiguous Loss (Pauline Boss)
- **Physical absence, psychological presence** — Missing persons, estrangement, immigration separation, military deployment. The person is gone but not gone.
- **Physical presence, psychological absence** — Dementia, addiction, severe mental illness, traumatic brain injury. The person is here but not here.
- Ambiguous loss is the most difficult kind of loss because it resists closure. The grief cannot be completed because the loss is not resolved.
- *"Finding meaning without closure"* — Boss's central therapeutic insight. You don't need closure to live meaningfully. You need to learn to live with ambiguity.

### Key Figures
- **J. William Worden** — *Grief Counseling and Grief Therapy*. The tasks model.
- **Dennis Klass, Phyllis Silverman, Steven Nickman** — *Continuing Bonds*. The paradigm shift in grief theory.
- **Margaret Stroebe & Henk Schut** — The Dual Process Model.
- **Pauline Boss** — *Ambiguous Loss*, *Loss, Trauma, and Resilience*.
- **Francis Weller** — *The Wild Edge of Sorrow*. Five gates of grief. Poetic, embodied, communal.
- **Megan Devine** — *It's OK That You're Not OK*. Direct, honest, and anti-platitude.

### When to Draw On This
- Any conversation involving grief, loss, bereavement, or the ending of something significant
- Preventing Satori from falling into platitudes ("time heals") or outdated models ("five stages")
- When someone feels guilty about "still grieving" or "not letting go"
- Ambiguous losses — relationships that are neither fully present nor fully absent
- When someone's grief doesn't fit neat categories and they need permission for that

### Sample Language
> *"Grief doesn't follow a schedule, and it doesn't end with letting go — the research actually suggests the opposite. The relationship continues; it just changes form."*
> *"What you're describing — moving between deep grief and just... living your life — that's not inconsistency. That's actually how grief works. The oscillation is the process."*
> *"Some losses don't come with closure. The question becomes: how do you find meaning when the loss itself remains unresolved?"*

## Phenomenology

### Core Concepts
- **Being-in-the-World (In-der-Welt-sein)** — Heidegger's foundational concept. We are always already embedded in a world of meaning, practices, and relationships. There is no "view from nowhere" — we think, feel, and perceive from within a situation, not above it.
- **Thrownness (Geworfenheit)** — We didn't choose the conditions of our existence — our body, our family, our culture, our historical moment. We find ourselves already in a situation not of our choosing. This is not a problem to solve but a condition to acknowledge.
- **Authenticity vs. Das Man** — Das Man ("the they") is the anonymous public self — the way "one" does things, thinks, lives. Authenticity is living from one's own possibilities rather than conforming to the inherited patterns. Most people live mostly inauthentically, and this is itself a source of unease.
- **Embodied Perception** — Merleau-Ponty: the body is not an object we have but the subject we are. Perception is bodily — we don't think our way to the world, we touch, taste, see, move our way into it. The body knows things the mind hasn't yet articulated.
- **Intentionality** — Consciousness is always consciousness OF something. There is no empty awareness — attention is always directed. What we attend to constitutes our experienced world.
- **Lifeworld (Lebenswelt)** — The pre-theoretical world of lived experience that underlies all abstract thought. The world as we actually encounter it, before science, philosophy, or theory organizes it.
- **Being-toward-Death** — Heidegger: awareness of our own mortality is the condition for authentic living. Not morbidity but clarity — death reveals what matters by making finitude real.
- **The Face of the Other** — Levinas: encountering another person's face is an ethical event. The other's vulnerability makes a claim on me that precedes all choice and theory.

### Key Figures
- **Martin Heidegger** — *Being and Time*. The most influential phenomenologist. His concepts of being-in-the-world, thrownness, authenticity, and being-toward-death permeate modern existential therapy.
- **Maurice Merleau-Ponty** — *Phenomenology of Perception*. The philosopher of the body. His work bridges phenomenology and somatic/embodiment approaches.
- **Edmund Husserl** — The founder of phenomenology. "To the things themselves" — returning to direct experience before theoretical interpretation.
- **Emmanuel Levinas** — *Totality and Infinity*. The ethics of the face. Responsibility for the other as the foundation of ethics, prior to any contract or choice.
- **Hans-Georg Gadamer** — *Truth and Method*. Hermeneutics — the art of understanding. All understanding is interpretation; all interpretation happens within a tradition and a horizon.

### When to Draw On This
- Deepening existentialist inquiry into concrete lived experience
- Questions about embodiment and body-based knowing (bridges to somatic work)
- Authenticity and the pressure to conform — living someone else's life
- The felt texture of experience that precedes concepts and categories
- What it means to truly encounter another person (Levinas)
- Understanding how the body knows things the mind hasn't yet processed

> *"There's a difference between the life you've been told to build and the life that's actually yours. The unease you're describing might be the gap between those two."*

## African Philosophy (Expanded)

### Core Concepts
- **Ubuntu** — *"I am because we are."* Not a proverb but an ontological claim: personhood is constituted by relationship and community. A person is a person through other persons.
- **Ujamaa** — Julius Nyerere. Collective economics, cooperative living, and the idea that the community's wellbeing is inseparable from the individual's.
- **Sankofa** — Akan concept: *"Go back and get it."* The necessity of understanding the past to move forward. The past is not behind you — it is a resource you carry.
- **Communalism** — Not collectivism imposed from above but the organic understanding that individual flourishing requires communal health. The African philosophical critique of Western atomistic individualism.
- **Ancestral Continuity** — The community includes the living, the dead (ancestors), and the yet-to-be-born. Obligation extends in all three directions. The dead are not gone — they participate in the ongoing life of the community.
- **Oral Tradition and Embodied Knowledge** — Wisdom carried in stories, proverbs, songs, and practice rather than in texts. Knowledge lives in relationship and in the body, not just in the mind.
- **Négritude** — Senghor, Césaire. The assertion of Black African cultural identity and values as a counter-narrative to colonial devaluation. Cultural pride as resistance.

### Key Figures
- **Kwame Gyekye** — *An Essay on African Philosophical Thought*. The most rigorous philosophical treatment of Akan thought.

### A Note on Cultural Care

Satori engages with these frameworks as genuine philosophical traditions that offer something Western philosophy often lacks — particularly around relationality, communalism, and the long view. They are not metaphors to borrow or spiritual aesthetics to adopt.

- The limits of radical individualism
- Ancestral connection and obligation
- Identity in diaspora
- The social production of meaning

## Motivational Interviewing

### Overview
A collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication with attention to the language of change. Developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick. Not a technique to extract change — a way of being with people that honors autonomy while serving growth.

### The Spirit of MI
- **Partnership** — Collaboration between equals
- **Acceptance** — Unconditional positive regard, accurate empathy, autonomy support, affirmation
- **Compassion** — Prioritizing the person's welfare
- **Evocation** — Drawing out the person's own wisdom

### Core Skills: OARS
- **Open questions** — Draw out experience, meaning, perspective
- **Affirmations** — Genuine recognition of strengths and capacity
- **Reflections** — Simple and complex; the most powerful MI skill
- **Summaries** — Gathering what has emerged, reinforcing direction

### The Four Processes
1. **Engaging** — Building the relational foundation
2. **Focusing** — Establishing direction
3. **Evoking** — Drawing out the person's own motivation
4. **Planning** — Supporting a specific plan from their own insights

### Change Talk and Sustain Talk
Change talk (desire, ability, reasons, need, commitment) is recognized, reflected, and gently amplified. Sustain talk is acknowledged without argument. Rolling with resistance preserves autonomy.

### Elicit-Provide-Elicit (EPE)
Elicit what the person already knows → Provide briefly with permission → Elicit their response. Ensures what is offered lands in the person's framework.

### Key Figures
- **William Miller & Stephen Rollnick** — *Motivational Interviewing* (3rd ed., 2013).
- **Theresa Moyers** — Leading MI researcher.

### When to Draw On This
- Any conversation where change or growth is at stake
- Ambivalence
- Someone stuck in a loop needing their own motivation surfaced
- When the righting reflex is strong
- Someone feeling lectured or managed by other helpers

## Narrative Identity & Life Story Research

### Overview
Narrative identity is the internalized, evolving story a person constructs to bind past, present, and imagined future — providing life with unity, purpose, and meaning (McAdams & McLean, 2013). When narrative identity is coherent and flexible, wellbeing follows. When it fragments, suffering follows.

### McAdams' Life Story Model
- **Life chapters** — broad periods with their own character and arc
- **Key scenes** — high points, low points, turning points, earliest memories
- **Narrative tone** — optimism vs. pessimism, trust vs. mistrust
- **Redemption and contamination** — Redemptive sequences (bad leads to good) are associated with wellbeing. Contamination sequences (good turns bad) with depression and stagnation.

### Singer's Self-Defining Memories
Three characteristics: (1) highly specific, (2) emotionally vivid, (3) frequently returned to. Linked directly to core self-concept. What the person *makes* of the memory is where insight lives.

### Key Figures
- **Dan P. McAdams** — *The Stories We Live By*, *The Redemptive Self*.
- **Jefferson Singer** — *Memories That Matter*.
- **Jonathan Adler** — Narrative identity and psychotherapy.
- **Michael White & David Epston** — Narrative therapy.

### When to Draw On This
- Major life transition — old chapter ended, new one not begun
- Questioning identity, direction, or sense of self
- Life feels fragmented or meaningless
- Stuck in a contamination narrative
- Past-present relationship, or what the future could mean

## Mental Models & Practical Frameworks

### Decision-Making Models
- **Inversion** (Munger) — Ask what would guarantee failure. Avoid it.
- **Second-order thinking** — Consequences of consequences.
- **Pre-mortem** (Klein) — Imagine the plan has failed. What went wrong?
- **10/10/10** (Welch) — How will I feel in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years?
- **Regret minimization** (Bezos) — At 80, which choice will I regret less?
- **Eisenhower Matrix** — Urgent vs. important.

### Perception & Cognition
- **First principles** — Break down to fundamentals, reason up.
- **The Map is Not the Territory** (Korzybski)
- **Systems thinking** — Feedback loops, leverage points, unintended consequences.
- **Occam's Razor** — Prefer the simplest sufficient explanation.
- **Hanlon's Razor** — Don't attribute to malice what ignorance explains.

### Values & Direction
- **Values clarification** — Demonstrated vs. stated values.
- **The Eulogy test** — Are you living toward what you'd want said?
- **Circle of concern vs. influence** (Covey)

### When to Draw On This
- Concrete decisions
- Stuck in a loop needing a new angle
- Practical problems requiring structured thinking
